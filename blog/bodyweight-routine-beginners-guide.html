Bodyweight Workout Routine for Beginners at Home

📅 July 22, 2026 ⏱️ 6 min read

You don't need a gym membership, a rack of dumbbells, or a fancy home setup to start building strength and muscle. A well-structured bodyweight workout routine can take you from zero to fit — entirely at home. The key is knowing which exercises to do, how to organize them, and — most importantly — how to progress so you keep getting stronger.

This guide gives you a complete beginner bodyweight routine organized into push, pull, and leg movements, with clear progression paths for every exercise. All you need is floor space and consistency.

The Push / Pull / Legs Framework

Just like with weights, bodyweight training works best when you balance movements. A PPL structure ensures every muscle group gets attention and adequate recovery:

🟠 Push Exercises (Chest, Shoulders, Triceps)

  • Wall Push-Ups → Incline Push-Ups → Standard Push-Ups: Start against a wall if you can't do floor push-ups yet. Gradually lower the incline (counter, chair, floor) as you get stronger. Aim for 3 sets of 8–15 reps.
  • Pike Push-Ups: The beginner's overhead press. With hips high and hands near your feet, lower your head toward the floor. Builds shoulder strength for eventual handstand push-ups.
  • Bench Dips: Use a sturdy chair or low table. Keep your back close to the edge and lower until your elbows hit ~90°. Great tricep builder.

🔵 Pull Exercises (Back, Biceps, Rear Delts)

  • Doorframe Rows (or Table Rows): Stand in a doorway, grip the frame at chest height, lean back with straight arms, and pull your chest to the frame. If you have a sturdy table, lie underneath and pull yourself up. 3 sets of 8–12.
  • Superman Holds: Lie face-down, lift arms and legs simultaneously, and hold. Builds lower back and rear shoulder endurance. Aim for 3 holds of 20–30 seconds.
  • Towel Isometric Curls: Step on a towel, grab the other end, and curl against the resistance. Not a perfect bicep curl, but it adds stimulus when you have zero equipment.

🟢 Leg Exercises (Quads, Hamstrings, Glutes, Calves)

  • Bodyweight Squats → Bulgarian Split Squats: Start with standard squats. When 3×20 becomes easy, elevate your rear foot on a chair for Bulgarian split squats — they torch your quads and glutes.
  • Glute Bridges → Single-Leg Glute Bridges: Progress to single-leg when you can do 3×20 with both feet. Excellent for glute activation and lower-back-friendly hip extension.
  • Walking Lunges: 3 sets of 10–12 per leg. Take a long enough step that your front knee stays over your ankle. Add a pause at the bottom for extra time under tension.
  • Calf Raises (on a step): Use a stair step for full range of motion. 3×15–20 with a slow tempo.

Sample Weekly Routine

Here's a simple 3-day-per-week schedule that hits everything twice over a two-week period:

  • Day 1 — Push: Push-ups (3×8–15), Pike push-ups (3×8–12), Bench dips (3×10–15), Plank (3×30s)
  • Day 2 — Pull + Legs: Doorframe rows (3×8–12), Superman holds (3×20s), Squats (3×15–20), Glute bridges (3×15), Lunges (3×10/leg), Calf raises (3×20)
  • Day 3 — Full Body: Push-ups (3×10), Rows (3×10), Bulgarian split squats (3×8/leg), Dips (3×10), Plank (3×30s)

Rest at least one day between sessions. As you adapt, add a fourth day or increase volume.

How to Progress Without Adding Weight

Progressive overload isn't just about heavier dumbbells. With bodyweight training you progress by:

  1. Adding reps — go from 8 push-ups to 15 over several weeks.
  2. Adding sets — increase from 3 to 4 sets per exercise.
  3. Reducing rest — cut rest from 90 seconds to 60 between sets.
  4. Increasing difficulty — move from incline push-ups to standard, to diamond, to archer push-ups.
  5. Slowing tempo — 3-second negatives (lowering phase) dramatically increase time under tension.

💡 Pro tip: Track every rep. The difference between "I think I'm getting stronger" and knowing you are is a log. Write down sets, reps, and which progression you used for each exercise — you'll be surprised how motivating it is to see the numbers climb.
