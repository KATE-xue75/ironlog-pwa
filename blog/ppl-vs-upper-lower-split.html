PPL vs Upper Lower Split: Which Training Split Is Better?

📅 July 21, 2026 ⏱️ 6 min read

If you've spent any time researching training programs, you've run into the PPL vs Upper/Lower debate. Both are popular, both produce results — but they work very differently. Choosing the wrong one for your schedule, recovery capacity, and goals can mean slower progress and unnecessary fatigue. Let's break both down so you can decide with confidence.

What Is a PPL Split?

Push/Pull/Legs (PPL) divides your training into three distinct days based on movement patterns:

  • Push day: Chest, shoulders, and triceps — bench press, overhead press, dips, tricep extensions.
  • Pull day: Back and biceps — deadlifts, pull-ups, rows, bicep curls, rear delts.
  • Legs day: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, and calves — squats, lunges, leg press, Romanian deadlifts.

Most people run PPL on a 6-day rotation (PPLPPL + one rest day), which means each muscle group gets trained twice per week. A less common variant is the 3-day PPL, but it only hits each group once weekly — far from ideal for hypertrophy.

What Is an Upper/Lower Split?

Upper/Lower splits the body into two categories:

  • Upper day: All upper-body muscles — chest, back, shoulders, biceps, triceps — in a single session.
  • Lower day: All lower-body muscles — quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves, and often core work.

The standard setup is a 4-day rotation: Upper, Lower, Rest, Upper, Lower, Rest, Rest. That's two sessions per muscle group per week — the same frequency as PPL but with one less gym day.

Head-to-Head Comparison

✅ PPL Strengths

  • High volume per muscle group per session
  • Shorter workouts (3–4 exercises per muscle)
  • Great for intermediate/advanced lifters
  • Easy to add specialization work

❌ PPL Weaknesses

  • Requires 6 days/week commitment
  • Leg day is a single grueling session
  • Hard to maintain with a busy schedule
  • Less flexible for missed days

✅ Upper/Lower Strengths

  • Only 4 days/week — more recovery
  • Great for beginners and intermediates
  • Easier to schedule around life
  • Full-body stimulus each Upper day

❌ Upper/Lower Weaknesses

  • Upper days can run long (60–90 min)
  • Less volume per muscle group per session
  • Hard to prioritize weak points
  • Can feel rushed on compound lifts

Who Should Choose PPL?

PPL shines for intermediate and advanced lifters who can commit to 5–6 gym sessions per week. If you love being in the gym, want to hit every muscle with focused volume, and have the recovery capacity to handle high-frequency training, PPL is the gold standard. Bodybuilders gravitate toward it for a reason — the per-session volume is unmatched.

Who Should Choose Upper/Lower?

Upper/Lower is ideal for beginners, busy professionals, and anyone prioritizing strength gains alongside muscle growth. Four days a week leaves room for cardio, sports, or just life. The higher per-session frequency for upper-body muscles (pressing and pulling in the same workout) also drives solid strength progress on compound lifts. If you've got 45–70 minutes four times a week, Upper/Lower delivers.

🔑 Key takeaway: Both splits work when you're consistent. PPL rewards time investment with more volume; Upper/Lower rewards efficiency with more recovery. Pick the one that fits your actual weekly schedule — not your ideal one.
